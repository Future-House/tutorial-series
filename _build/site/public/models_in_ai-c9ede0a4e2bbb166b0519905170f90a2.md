# AI Models Commonly Used in Biology


## First things first, what is a "model"?

An AI model is simply an algorithm that has been trained on data in order to recognize patterns and make predictions. The data used to train a model typically consists of examples. Each example may contain:

- an **input** (e.g., a protein sequence, microscopy image, gene expression profile)

- optionally a **label** describing the correct answer (e.g., the protein's function, the cell type in an image, or whether a mutation is pathogenic)

During training, the model examines many such examples and gradually learns statistical relationships between the inputs and outputs. 
Once trained, the model can then be used to make predictions on new data it has never seen before. For instance, it might predict the function of a newly discovered protein or identify structures in a microscopy image.


It is also useful to distinguish between **supervised** and **self-supervised** learning.

In supervised learning, the training data includes both inputs and labels. The model learns by comparing its predictions to the correct answers and adjusting itself to reduce errors. For example, a dataset might contain protein sequences along with experimentally determined functions, and the model learns to predict the function from the sequence.

In self-supervised learning, the model learns patterns from data without explicit labels. Instead, part of the data is hidden or modified and the model learns to predict the missing information. For instance, a model trained on protein sequences might learn to predict masked amino acids within sequences. By solving this task across millions of sequences, the model learns useful representations of proteins that can later be applied to many biological problems.


Computational modeling is foundational to modern biology and science, spanning a continuum from first-principles physics-based simulations to data-driven machine learning and, most recently, large-scale foundation models. This report provides an organized survey of the major categories of AI and computational models encountered in biological research, with explanations of their principles, architectures, applications, and key examples.

## 2. Physics-Based Models

Physics-based models encode known physical laws, conservation principles, and mechanistic understanding directly into mathematical formulations. They are the oldest class of computational models in biology and remain indispensable for problems where mechanistic interpretability is paramount.

### 2.1 Ordinary and Partial Differential Equations (ODEs/PDEs)

Ordinary differential equations are the most common approach in systems biology, treating biological systems as networks of chemical reactions whose concentrations change over time ([1.1]). ODEs model temporal dynamics of gene regulatory networks, signaling cascades, and metabolic pathways. However, basic ODE formulations have limitations for spatial transport, diffusion, and stochastic effects, which motivates the use of partial differential equations (PDEs) when spatial and temporal dependencies must be captured simultaneously, such as in reaction–diffusion systems and tissue-level modeling ([2.1]). Extensions to stochastic differential equations capture intrinsic noise and variability important when molecule numbers are small ([2.2]). Numerical solution methods include Runge–Kutta and implicit methods, and specialized solvers such as LSODA and CVODE handle stiff systems ([1.1]). Simulation platforms such as VCell and E-Cell support these formalisms ([1.1]).

### 2.2 Molecular Dynamics and Force Fields

At the molecular scale, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations compute the time evolution of atomic positions using Newtonian or Langevin equations of motion, with forces derived from empirical force fields that include electrostatic, van der Waals, and covalent bond terms ([3.1]). Atomistic MD provides detailed insight into protein folding, conformational dynamics, and protein–ligand binding, and has reached experimentally relevant timescales for large systems ([4.1]). Coarse-grained methods reduce computational cost by grouping atoms into effective interaction sites, enabling studies at larger length and time scales ([4.1]). Multiscale methods combine different levels of theory—from quantum mechanical electronic structure calculations through atomistic MD to continuum descriptions—to connect chemical and molecular changes to macroscopic observables ([4.1]).

### 2.3 Agent-Based and Cell-Based Models

Agent-based models (ABMs) simulate individual cells as discrete agents interacting in virtual tissues, allowing researchers to explore how single-cell behaviors give rise to tissue-scale dynamics ([5.1]). Spatial frameworks include lattice-based methods (cellular automata, Cellular Potts models) and off-lattice approaches (center-based models, subcellular-element models) ([5.2]). Many ABMs are hybrid: discrete cell agents are coupled to continuum PDE solvers for diffusive transport of oxygen, drugs, and growth factors ([5.2]). PhysiCell is a prominent open-source physics-based multicellular simulator that includes sub-models for cell cycling, apoptosis, necrosis, mechanics, and motility, and is coupled to BioFVM for biotransport ([2.3]). Other toolkits include Chaste and CompuCell3D ([5.2]). These models are widely used for studying tumor growth, angiogenesis, cancer immunology, and for pre-screening therapeutic strategies ([5.1]).

## 3. Traditional Machine Learning Models

Traditional (non-deep-learning) machine learning methods remain the recommended starting point for many biological tasks, as they are faster to develop, easier to interpret, and often appropriate when datasets are modest in size ([6.1], [6.2]).

### 3.1 Support Vector Machines (SVMs)

SVMs learn optimal separating hyperplanes for classification and can handle non-linear decision boundaries through kernel functions (RBF, polynomial, sigmoid) ([7.1]). In biology, SVMs have been applied to protein function and transmembrane topology prediction, microarray-based cancer classification, and toxicological response modeling ([6.3], [8.1]). SVMs can perform both classification and regression but may be difficult to scale to very large datasets ([6.3]).

### 3.2 Random Forests (RF)

Random forests are ensemble methods that combine many decision trees via bagging and random feature subsampling to reduce overfitting and bias ([7.1]). RF is particularly valued in biology because it provides feature importance estimates, requires relatively little hyperparameter tuning, and is robust to noise ([6.1], [6.3]). Applications include genome-wide association studies (GWAS) for detecting SNP–SNP interactions, protein–protein interaction prediction, microRNA precursor classification (e.g., MiPred), scoring protein–ligand interactions, and predicting disease-associated mutations ([9.1], [10.1], [6.3]).

### 3.3 Other Classical Methods

k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) is an instance-based, nonparametric method useful as a simple baseline classifier for biological data ([7.1]). Regularized linear regression methods (Ridge, LASSO, Elastic Net) are applied to protein variant effect prediction and biochemical kinetics; they are easy to interpret but cannot learn complex feature relationships ([6.3]). Clustering methods (k-means, DBSCAN) and dimensionality reduction techniques (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) are widely used for discovery and visualization, particularly in single-cell transcriptomics ([6.1]). The history of ML in biology extends back to early perceptron models used to identify translation start sites in *E. coli* ([11.1]).

## 4. Deep Learning Models

Deep learning methods, based on artificial neural networks with multiple layers, have transformed computational biology by their ability to automatically learn hierarchical representations from raw data ([12.1]). Deep learning is most advantageous when large datasets, many features, or highly structured inputs are available ([6.2]).

### 4.1 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

CNNs capture local spatial patterns through convolutional filters and pooling operations and are well suited for image-like biological data ([7.2]). In biology, CNNs are applied to histopathology and biomedical image analysis, protein structure prediction using co-evolutionary residue-pair information, DNA/RNA motif and variant identification, cryo-EM image analysis, and medical image classification ([6.4]). DeepBind and DeepCpG are notable examples for RNA-binding site and DNA methylation prediction, respectively ([13.1]). CNNs have also been widely applied to gene expression analysis and sequence-based tasks ([12.2]).

### 4.2 Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs

RNNs are designed for sequential data and have been applied to biological sequences and time series. LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) and GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit) variants address the vanishing gradient problem, enabling capture of long-range dependencies ([7.2]). Applications include protein secondary structure prediction, promoter detection, temporal gene expression modeling, peptide design, and clinical diagnosis prediction from health records ([6.4]). RNNs have also been used for DNA sequence analysis and predicting translation from mRNA ([7.2], [13.2]).

### 4.3 Generative Models: GANs and Autoencoders

Generative adversarial networks (GANs) and variational autoencoders (VAEs) are used for synthetic data generation, data augmentation, latent-space modeling, and unsupervised exploration of biological data distributions ([7.2]). In biology, these models support synthetic biology and gene synthesis, protein design, single-cell denoising and imputation, metabolomics analysis, and DNA motif discovery ([12.2], [12.3]).

### 4.4 Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

GNNs extend neural network methods to graph-structured data, enabling learning on molecular graphs, protein interaction networks, and cellular graphs ([7.2]). Applications include drug–target interaction and drug response prediction, drug repurposing, protein–protein interaction network analysis, and single-cell RNA-seq analyses for cell-type classification and gene regulation modeling ([12.2], [13.3]). scGNN is a notable example for single-cell transcriptomics ([13.3]).

### 4.5 Transformers

Transformer architectures, originally developed for natural language processing, use self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range dependencies in sequences without the sequential processing bottleneck of RNNs ([7.2]). In biology, transformers have been applied to protein structure prediction (e.g., AlphaFold), genomic sequence analysis, RNA structure prediction, protein contact prediction, and drug–target interaction modeling ([12.2], [13.1]). The Molecule Transformer-Drug Target Interaction (MT-DTI) model is one example trained on chemical and amino acid sequences for DTI prediction ([13.3]).

## 5. Foundation Models

Foundation models (FMs) represent a paradigm shift in computational biology. They are large-scale AI systems pre-trained on vast, often unlabeled datasets using self-supervised or generative objectives, and then adapted to diverse downstream tasks via fine-tuning, few-shot, or zero-shot transfer ([14.1]). FMs address longstanding challenges in bioinformatics, including limited annotated data, data noise, and the need for generalizable representations across biological domains ([15.1], [15.2]).

### 5.1 Protein Language Models

Protein language models treat amino acid sequences analogously to natural language text, applying masked language modeling or autoregressive generation to learn rich sequence representations. ESM-2 is a family of BERT-style transformer encoders scaled up to 15 billion parameters, trained on over 60 million protein sequences from UniRef ([16.1], [16.2]). As models scale, they learn information sufficient for atomic-level structure prediction; ESM-2's internal representations correlate strongly with protein structure, and attention maps capture protein contact information ([16.3]). ESMFold leverages frozen ESM-2 representations with a folding trunk adapted from AlphaFold2's structure module, replacing MSA-dependent components with self-attention over language model features ([17.1], [16.4]). ESMFold achieves TM-scores of 0.83 on CAMEO benchmarks and processes proteins approximately 60× faster than AlphaFold2 by eliminating the need for multiple sequence alignments ([17.1], [17.2]). Using ESMFold, the ESM Metagenomic Atlas was generated, characterizing over 617 million structures including millions of novel folds ([17.2]).

AlphaFold2 combines an Evoformer module that processes MSA and template information with an equivariant structure module to produce 3D coordinates, achieving near-experimental accuracy (TM-scores of 0.88 on CAMEO) ([17.1], [17.3]). It has been described as an unsupervised FM using self-distillation-like techniques and has catalyzed the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database covering over 214 million protein sequences ([14.2]).

Generative protein models such as ProtGPT2 (GPT-style autoregressive generation) and ProGen (conditional language modeling with taxonomic/functional control tags) enable de novo protein sequence design beyond natural evolutionary constraints ([14.2], [14.3]).

### 5.2 Genomic Foundation Models

Genomic FMs apply language-model-style pre-training to DNA sequences. DNABERT uses k-mer masked language modeling to learn bidirectional representations for tasks such as promoter identification and transcription factor binding site prediction, while DNABERT-2 improves efficiency through byte-pair encoding tokenization and multi-species training ([14.4], [15.2]). Enformer is a supervised FM with a long receptive field that captures long-range enhancer–promoter interactions and gene expression patterns from sequence ([14.5]). HyenaDNA uses a Hyena hierarchy (long convolution architecture) with context windows up to one million tokens, enabling modeling of very long-range genomic patterns ([14.5]). The Nucleotide Transformer builds and pre-trains foundational language models across genomic datasets at various parameter scales ([14.4]).

### 5.3 Single-Cell Foundation Models

The emergence of large single-cell RNA sequencing datasets has enabled foundation models for single-cell biology. scGPT uses generative pre-training on over 10 million single-cell profiles for tasks including cell-type annotation, perturbation response prediction, and batch correction ([15.2], [14.5]). Geneformer is pre-trained on approximately 30 million single-cell transcriptomes using rank-value encodings and captures gene–gene interactions useful for modeling dosage sensitivity and chromatin dynamics ([14.5]). General-purpose large cellular models (LCMs) such as scFoundation are pre-trained via self-supervised objectives and have demonstrated strong performance across cell clustering, cell-type annotation, and multi-omics integration ([14.3], [15.2]).

## 6. Hybrid and Integrative Approaches

### 6.1 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs integrate physical laws directly into deep learning by embedding differential equation residuals and physical constraints (e.g., conservation laws, boundary conditions) into the neural network loss function alongside data-fitting terms ([18.1], [18.2]). This enables the network to solve both forward problems (approximating ODE/PDE solutions) and inverse problems (estimating unknown parameters from data). Applications in biology include tumor growth modeling using the Gompertz curve, gene expression kinetics described by translation and degradation ODEs, and epidemiological SIR models ([18.1], [18.3]). PINNs offer advantages for sparse or noisy data scenarios but are sensitive to network architecture and hyperparameter choices ([18.2]).

### 6.2 Differentiable Biology

Differentiable biology represents the broader paradigm of combining differentiable programming with mechanistic models. As defined by AlQuraishi and Sorger, differentiable biology applies differentiable programs—learnable models with defined derivatives—to model molecular and cellular phenomena ([19.1]). These programs combine domain-specific mathematical equations with general-purpose machine-learnable components, optimized end-to-end using automatic differentiation frameworks such as PyTorch, TensorFlow, and JAX ([19.1]). The approach provides three classes of primitives: biological pattern recognizers (CNNs, GCNs, transformers), physical/phenomenological priors (symmetries, conservation laws), and experimental/data-acquisition priors ([19.2]). Differentiable simulators can be embedded within learning frameworks, enabling neural networks to leverage high-accuracy physics-based predictions at reduced computational cost ([19.3]). This paradigm unifies physics-based and data-driven approaches and has been applied to problems ranging from protein folding to molecular binding kinetics ([19.2], [19.3]).

## 7. Conclusions

The landscape of AI models in biology and science spans a rich continuum from physics-based mechanistic models grounded in differential equations, force fields, and agent-based rules, through classical machine learning methods that remain effective baselines for many problems, to deep learning architectures (CNNs, RNNs, GNNs, transformers, generative models) that excel at learning from large and complex datasets, and finally to foundation models that leverage massive pre-training to achieve unprecedented generalization across biological domains. Emerging hybrid approaches—particularly physics-informed neural networks and differentiable biology—represent a promising convergence of mechanistic understanding and data-driven flexibility, offering the potential to build models that are simultaneously interpretable, accurate, and scalable. As biological data continue to grow in volume and complexity, the integration of these diverse modeling paradigms will be essential for advancing scientific discovery.
